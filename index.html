About

My research sits at the intersection of farm financial management and conservation agriculture. Working with Kansas Farm Management Association (KFMA) panel data and field-level precision agriculture records, I study how practices like no-till and cover cropping shape farm profitability, credit access, and resilience to weather and cash-flow shocks. I also contribute to applied policy work on the farm safety net, including analysis of commodity programs and crop insurance under recent legislative changes.

I'm advised by Dr. Jennifer Ifft in the Department of Agricultural Economics at Kansas State University. Before K-State, I earned my undergraduate degree at Zamorano University (Escuela Agrícola Panamericana) in Honduras. Before the numbers, I care about the question every producer eventually asks: does this practice pay on my operation? Much of my work is about answering that honestly — separating cost savings from yield effects, correlation from causation, and enthusiasm from evidence.

Active Research

Conference paper · AAEA 2026, Kansas City

Farm Debt Dynamics Following Cash Flow Shocks

with Gaku and Jennifer Ifft — KFMA panel, 2013–2020

Uses a rolling z-score shock framework to trace how weather and cash-flow shocks move through farm balance sheets.

Accumulated heat stress significantly reduces current and total loan volume, with the largest credit contraction on high-leverage farms.

Dissertation project · In development

Turning Farmers' Own Prescription Maps into Causal Experiments

Spatial regression discontinuity using variable-rate seeding boundaries

Prescription zone boundaries create spatial discontinuities where input rates jump while soil characteristics change smoothly — a natural experiment embedded in ordinary farm operations. A proof-of-concept on a 2024 corn field shows a strong first stage and passes falsification tests.

Does Conservation Agriculture Increase Resilience to Adverse Weather? Field-Level Evidence

with Margaret Jodlowski and Jennifer Ifft

Field-level evidence on whether conservation agriculture buffers yields and returns against adverse weather.

Working paper

Financial Constraints and Conservation Adoption

KFMA panel, 2002–2022 — 3,339 farms

Asks whether financial capacity enables no-till adoption or whether financial benefits flow from it. Agronomic conditions explain adoption far better than balance sheets do.

Extension & Outreach

Healthy Soil, Healthy Bottom Line is an extension program I lead under K-State Regenerative Agriculture, translating peer-reviewed findings on soil health economics into decision tools for producers. Through four in-person delivery events, the program has reached more than 220 Kansas producers, with pre/post evaluation benchmarked against the original KFMA study sample.

The program was developed for the 2026 AAEA Extension Competition and continues as an ongoing outreach effort.
