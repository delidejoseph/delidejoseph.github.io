Conference Papers

Farm Debt Dynamics Following Cash Flow Shocks
with Gaku and Jennifer Ifft · AAEA Annual Meeting 2026, Kansas City
Abstract

Weather-induced cash flow shocks pose significant risks to agricultural producers through their effects on crop yields, farm income, and financial decision-making. This study examines how Kansas commercial farms respond to weather-driven cash flow shocks—both contemporaneous and persistent—using farm-level panel data from the Kansas Farm Management Association (KFMA) and county-level temperature data from PRISM for the period 2013 to 2020. We find that exposure to extreme degree days above 32◦C (EDD) increases total loan volume and long-term borrowing, consistent with producers using debt to manage operational costs and minimize rollover risk following cash flow shocks. We extend this framework by examining consecutive shock exposure—classifying farm-years by their position within multi-year above-threshold heat streaks. Farms in the first consecutive shock year significantly increase current borrowing, while farms in the second consecutive shock year significantly increase machinery investment, revealing a two-stage adaptation sequence invisible to single-year analysis. Young producers are unable to access current credit in consecutive shock years at the same rate as the average farm, consistent with tighter credit constraints for operators with shorter financial histories. No-till adopters expand their total credit relationships in the onset year of a consecutive streak, suggesting that conservation tillage signals balance sheet quality to lenders under recurring heat stress. These findings are robust to Conley spatial standard errors and inverse hyperbolic sine transformation of outcomes. Our results indicate that producers use external finance to navigate weather induced cash flow shocks, with the persistence of these shocks generating distinct financial adaptation responses beyond what contemporaneous heat intensity alone predicts..

Impact of Trade Facilitation on Global Agricultural Trade: Evidence from a Structural Gravity Model
with Allen Featherstone
Abstract

This paper provides new evidence on the trade-flow effects of agricultural-specific and digital trade facilitation measures by estimating a structural gravity model via Poisson pseudo-maximum likelihood (PPML) on a panel of 135 economies across four survey rounds (2017, 2019, 2021, and 2023). Implementation rate data from the United Nations Global Survey on Digital and Sustainable Trade Facilitation serve as direct measures of policy inputs, rather than outcome-based proxies such as border-crossing times or logistics costs, and are used to construct geometric-mean bilateral indices for general, paperless, and agricultural trade facilitation, merged with bilateral agricultural trade data from UN COMTRADE via WITS. Three-way fixed effects — exporter-time, importer-time, and country-pair — absorb multilateral resistance terms and all time-invariant bilateral heterogeneity, with identification coming from within-pair variation in implementation rates over time. A 10-percent improvement in the bilateral agricultural trade facilitation index is associated with a statistically significant 0.22–0.26 percent increase in bilateral agricultural exports, robust to the exclusion of general and paperless facilitation controls. General and paperless trade facilitation yield no independent effect once agricultural-specific measures are controlled for. Disaggregation of the composite index into its four component measures reveals that SPS testing and laboratory infrastructure (UN Survey measure 44) exerts the largest individual trade effect, followed by national standards and accreditation bodies (measure 45), e-application and issuance of SPS certificates (measure 46), and special treatment for perishable goods (measure 47). Among South–South pairs, trade digitalization emerges as the dominant driver of agricultural trade, pointing to large untapped gains from regional paperless trade integration among developing economies. These findings carry direct implications for aid-for-trade prioritization: investments in SPS testing infrastructure and e-SPS certification, where the North–South implementation gap reaches 38 percentage points, offer the highest returns for expanding developing-country participation in global agricultural trade.

Does Conservation Agriculture Increase Resilience to Adverse Weather? Field-Level Evidence
with Margaret Jodlowski and Jennifer Ifft
Abstract

Agricultural conservation practices have the potential to increase crops’ resilience to extreme weather shocks, but there is limited evidence of these benefits outside of research trials or case studies. In this study, we explore whether farm level outcomes from the Federal crop insurance program (FCIP) can advance understanding of the degree to which common conservation practices increase resilience. This FCIP is a key part of crop farms’ risk management strategies and participation is nearly universal. We observe farm-level production practices, insurance use, and insurance payouts in the USDA’s Agricultural Resource Management Survey (ARMS), a multi-year, nation ally representative field and farm-level survey of major field crops. We first consider evidence for how behavioral factors may influence both crop insurance purchase levels and conservation practice adoption. We find that crop insurance use has a varied relationship with conservation practices, which may in some cases influence insurance outcomes. Our primary estimating equations test whether farms using specific tillage practices or cover crops have lower likelihoods and amounts of crop insurance indemnity payouts after exposure to atypical growing season weather. For practices that are unrelated or positively related to crop insurance choices, we find suggestive evidence of lower crop insurance payouts in years with excess rainfall. Conservation practices may increase resilience to weather shocks, but analysis of policy outcomes to assess this relationship should account for how risk management and conservation choices may be jointly determined.

Extension & Outreach

Healthy Soil, Healthy Bottom Line is an extension program I lead under K-State Regenerative Agriculture, translating peer-reviewed findings on soil health economics into decision tools for producers. Through four in-person delivery events, the program has reached more than 220 Kansas producers, with pre/post evaluation benchmarked against the original KFMA study sample.

The program will be presented at the 2026 AAEA Extension Competition and continues as an ongoing outreach effort.
